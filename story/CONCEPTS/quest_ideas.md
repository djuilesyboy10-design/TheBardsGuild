This is a fantastic angle for a "Bards Lore" mod. In The Elder Scrolls, the concept of Unreliable Narrators and Dragon Breaks (where time shatters and all possibilities become true at once) means there are many "discarded" versions of history.

Here are three scenarios based on real lore contradictions where the "spirits" you find are trapped in a version of history that Bethesda later changed or retconned.

1. The Bird Men: "The Infinite Treaty"
The Fragment: In a misty, floating island pocket of Oblivion, the player finds a group of translucent, feather-clad humanoids (the Bird Men) standing before a phantom version of Topal the Pilot.

The Loop: Every few minutes, they perform a beautiful, complex "Wing-Dance" and sing a song that vibrates the very ground. They are reenacting the moment they handed over Cyrodiil. However, because they are in Oblivion, the song is becoming distorted—turning into a screech.

The Bard's Role: The player realizes that if the song becomes fully distorted, the "memory" of the Bird Men will vanish from Mundus forever. You must use your own instrument to "harmonize" with them, hitting the right notes to stabilize the loop and record the melody before the Daedric influence corrupts it.

2. The Wraxu: "The Eternal Charge"
The Fragment: In a realm resembling a never-ending, blood-red swamp, a massive Wraxu (Giant Bird) is locked in a permanent battle against a phantasmal Imperial Legion.

The Loop: The bird lets out a world-shaking cry, charges the line of soldiers, and is brought down by a volley of ghost-arrows, only to vanish and reappear at the start of the charge seconds later. It is a "Living War-Canto" trapped in a cycle of defeat.

The Bard's Role: The bird isn't just fighting; its heartbeat is a drum rhythm. The player must stand in the center of the battlefield and use a drum to match the Wraxu’s heartbeat. Doing so "sets the rhythm" of the loop, allowing the bird to finally break the cycle or let out one final, pure note that the player can "capture" in a soul gem or a scroll.

1. The Jungle of Cyrodiil (The "Retcon" Loop)
The Contradiction: In Morrowind (TES III), books like The Provinces of Tamriel describe Cyrodiil as a "vast endless jungle." But in Oblivion (TES IV), it is a European-style forest/plain. The lore reason given later was that Tiber Septim used magic to change the landscape throughout time.

The Scenario: You enter an Oblivion pocket where the spirits are Imperial Legionnaires from the 2nd Era. They are drowning in mud and fighting giant insects in a thick, tropical rainforest.

The Conflict: These spirits are terrified because they can feel the "new" history (the plains/forests) trying to overwrite them. They believe they are the real history and that the player (who comes from a world where Cyrodiil is a forest) is a hallucination or a demon of "The Lie."

The Scenario: In a dark, cavernous realm, you find a group of spirits that look like Orcs but act like primitive, mindless "Goblin-ken."

The Conflict: These spirits are "Non-Canon Orcs." They are trapped because they exist in a timeline where they were just monsters, not the "Orsimer" (Elven Orcs) players know today. They are being hunted by a spectral Topal the Pilot. You have to decide: do you help them find their "Elven" soul, or do you let them remain the "Monsters" history says they weren't?

The Conflict: The spirits are flickering between being a "Beast Race" and "Elves." They are trapped in a loop of identity crisis because the "official" history can't decide what they were. 1. The "Mananauts" of the Battlespire (Space-Travel Paradox)
The Contradiction: Early lore (and the game Battlespire) describes the Empire as having Mananauts and "Void-ships" that traveled to the moons (Masser and Secunda). In modern games like Skyrim, this is almost never mentioned, making the Empire seem like a standard medieval fantasy setting rather than a magically advanced space-faring one.

The Scenario: In a pocket of Oblivion that looks like a shattered glass tower in the stars, you find spirits in "Star-Suit" armor. They are frantically trying to calculate the distance to the moon using giant brass astrolabes.

The Loop: They are trapped in the moment their ship "crashed" because the modern history of Tamriel stopped believing in them. They are literally being erased by the "gravity" of the new, less-magical lore.

Bard’s Role: You must play a "Song of the Spheres" to help their spirits finally reach the moon in death, proving that the Empire's reach once touched the stars.

2. The "Ebonarm" Paradox (The Deleted God)
The Contradiction: In Daggerfall, there was a major god named Reymon Ebonarm, the God of War who hated all Daedra. He was hugely popular, but after Daggerfall, he was almost completely scrubbed from the lore by Bethesda. He essentially became a "deleted" deity.

The Scenario: You find a lone, massive warrior in black ebony armor with a sword fused to his hand, sitting in a field of red roses (his symbol) within a void. He is surrounded by ghostly worshippers who are slowly fading into smoke.

The Loop: He asks the player, "Why can no one say my name?" He is a god who cannot die but no longer exists in the "official" pantheon.

Bard’s Role: The Bard must learn his name and promise to sing it in the taverns of Tamriel. Even if the world forgets, the song keeps the spirit of the "Forgotten God" alive.

3. The "Khajiiti Man" (The Ohmes-raht Retcon)
The Contradiction: In Arena, the Khajiit looked exactly like Humans with face paint. In later games, they became the cat-people we know. While later lore explained this through "lunar phases," the transition in the early games felt like a complete visual retcon.

The Scenario: You find a tribe of spirits that look entirely human, claiming to be Khajiit from the Merethic Era. They are being mocked and "hunted" by spirits of modern, cat-like Khajiit who call them "shaved pretenders."

The Loop: They are trapped in a loop of trying to prove their "cat-hood" despite looking like Men. It’s a tragic, bizarre scene of identity crisis.

Bard’s Role: You use your music to bridge the gap between the "Man-Khajiit" and the "Cat-Khajiit," performing the Dance of the Two Moons to show that both forms are valid parts of the same ancient story.
